Exploring the Forces of Nature ----------------------------------

Natural disasters, such as Volcanic Explosions, Tsunamisand Earthquakes, have the power to reshape our world in the blink of an eye. These awe-inspiring yet often destructive phenomena remind us of the immense forces at play beneath the Earth's surface and within our planet's dynamic systems. In this visualization, we embark on a journey to explore the impact of these natural disasters, their geographic distribution, and the historical significance they hold

--------------- Volcanoes ---------------

This map details volcanic eruptions (2010-2018) with VEI and lava elevation, revealing global patterns.

VEI (Volcanic Explosivity Index) quantifies eruption power from 0 (non-explosive) to 8 (catastrophic) based on ejected material volume, aiding eruption classification and hazard assessment.

Elevation (in m), volcanic eruptions propel lava to varying heights influenced by eruption factors like magma composition and gas presence, aiding eruption analysis.

Different Types of Volcanoes

Different Types of Volcanoes that have caused explosions around the world between 2010-2018 are Caldera, Complex Volcano, Lava Dome, Shield Volcano, Stratovolcano and Subglacial Volcano. Their distribution can be seen in the donut chart and Stacked bar chart above.

--------------- Tsunami ---------------

Maximum water height during a tsunami, or tsunami wave height measures the highest seawater point reached during a tsunami event, crucial for effective warning systems and coastal safety planning


Earthquake magnitude,, often measured on the Richter scale, directly influences tsunami generation. Greater magnitudes result in more potent undersea disturbances and larger, potentially devastating tsunamis

--------------------- Earthquakes ---------------------

The focal depth of an earthquake, known as hypocenter depth indicates where the quake originates within the Earth's crust.

Earthquake magnitude is a measure of seismic energy released during a quake. The Richter scale and moment magnitude scale quantify the earthquake's size and potential for damage.
